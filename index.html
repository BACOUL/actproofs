ActProofs is the open standard for cryptographic, portable, and independently verifiable proof that an automated or AI-driven action was explicitly authorized.

Logs, policies, and workflows are not evidence.

Read the ActProofs Standard Regulatory context

Logs are not proof.

Execution traces cannot demonstrate intent, approval, or responsibility.

The problem

Implicit authorization

Automated systems and AI agents trigger actions with real-world impact, but authorization decisions are rarely explicit or provable.

Execution ≠ approval

Logs, policies, and workflows describe what happened — not who approved it, when, and under which responsibility.

Audit dead-end

After incidents or disputes, organizations cannot independently demonstrate that an automated action was explicitly authorized.

Why ActProofs exists

Regulators, auditors, insurers, and governance teams increasingly ask a simple question:

“Who authorized this automated action, and when?”

Today, there is no portable, cryptographic, and independently verifiable proof of authorization — only internal records.

ActProofs fills this gap.

How it works

ActProofs records authorization decisions as evidence — independently from execution or enforcement.

1. Declare the action

Describe the intended automated action, its scope, and its context before execution.

2. Issue an ActProof

A cryptographic authorization proof is generated, binding the action, the authorizing entity, and a timestamp.

3. Verify independently

The proof can be verified at any time, online or offline, without trusting the issuer.

Authorization without proof is liability.

ActProofs exists to close this governance gap.

What ActProofs is — and is not

What it is

✔ Proof of authorization decisions

✔ Cryptographic, portable evidence

✔ Stateless and verification-first

✔ Governance and compliance building block

What it is not

✘ Not execution or enforcement

✘ Not identity or access management

✘ Not workflow orchestration

✘ Not logging or monitoring

ActProofs records authorization decisions only.

Relation to TimeProofs

TimeProofs provides cryptographic proof that a digital artifact or event existed at a specific point in time.

ActProofs provides cryptographic proof that an automated action was explicitly authorized by a responsible entity.

The two standards are complementary: existence versus authorization.
