Execution logs are not proof of authorization

Logs, policies, and workflow traces describe what happened. They do not prove who authorized an action, when, or under which responsibility.

ActProofs exists to provide a missing layer: provable authorization, independent from execution and durable over time.

The problem

Implicit authorization

Automated systems and AI agents increasingly trigger actions with real-world consequences, yet authorization decisions are often implicit, undocumented, or impossible to prove afterward.

After an incident, dispute, or regulatory inquiry, organizations cannot independently demonstrate that a specific automated action was explicitly authorized beforehand.

How it works

Three steps: declare ‚Üí authorize ‚Üí verify

1) Declare the action

Describe the intended automated action, its scope, its context, and the responsible entity.

2) Issue an ActProof

A cryptographic authorization proof is generated, binding the declared action, the authorizing actor, and a timestamp.

3) Verify independently

Anyone can verify the ActProof at any time, online or offline, without trusting the issuer.

Authorization evidence is independent from execution and remains verifiable over time.
